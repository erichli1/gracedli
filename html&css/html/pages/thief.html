Portrait of a Thief

This was how things began: Boston on the cusp of fall, the Sackler Museum robbed of 23 pieces of priceless Chinese art. Even in this back room, dust catching the slant of golden, late- afternoon light, Will Chen could hear the sirens. They sounded like a promise.
History is told by the conquerors. Will Chen is an art history major at Harvard, and has spent most of his life learning about the West—its art, its culture, all that it has taken and called its own. Even so, when a Chinese corporation offers him a chance to reclaim the art China lost centuries ago, it should feel like the end to everything he hopes to be—a perfect student, a responsible older brother, the eldest son who has always been his parents’ American Dream.

Instead, it feels like a beginning.

The job is clear, simple, impossible: five museums, five sculptures stolen from Beijing’s Old Summer Palace centuries ago. His crew is equally improbable: a public policy major at Duke who can talk her way out of anything, a premed with steady hands and dreams of being a surgeon, an engineering student who races cars in her spare time, and an MIT dropout turned Silicon Valley software engineer. Group projects don’t come easily to any of them, but for this—millions of dollars, a chance to make history—they can’t afford to fail.

PORTRAIT OF A THIEF is Ocean’s Eleven meets The Farewell, and is based on this true story of Chinese art vanishing from Western museums. As someone who grew up watching heist movies, from the Italian Job to the Fast and Furious franchise, I’ve always wanted to write a classic heist story, with car chases and museum break-ins, but with characters that reflected my own experiences. This is a heist story, but it’s also a story of diaspora, art, and the complexity of the Chinese American identity.
